## Workshop Setup {#workshop-setup}


# Workshop Setup Guide

Read this at least **one week before** the workshop begins.

## 1. Software

- **Microsoft Excel** (2016 or newer, or Microsoft 365) on every learner's laptop.
  - If a learner only has a phone: the free **Excel mobile app** works for Level 1 sessions 1–4, but Level 2 (PivotTables, Slicers) needs a laptop or desktop.
  - No Excel license? Google Sheets can substitute for Level 1, but tell learners clearly that some button names will look different.
- Confirm every laptop opens Excel **before** Day 1. Do not assume — test it in a WhatsApp group chat the week before.

## 2. Room / venue

- Power sockets for every 2 learners (extension boards are your best friend — expect NEPA/power cuts, so a generator or inverter backup is strongly recommended).
- Tables where learners can see each other's screens for pair work.
- A projector or large screen for live demonstrations.
- Internet access is a "nice to have," not a requirement — all datasets are provided offline as CSV files.

## 3. Materials checklist

- [ ] One laptop per learner (or per pair, minimum)
- [ ] Practice dataset CSVs loaded onto every laptop (from the [datasets folder](datasets/))
- [ ] Printed or shared copy of the [Practice Workbook Structure](workbook-structure.md)
- [ ] Attendance sheet
- [ ] Name tags (first names only, large and bold — many learners are shy to ask for names again)
- [ ] Extension boards / power strips
- [ ] Projector + adapter cables (HDMI and VGA — don't assume which one the venue has)
- [ ] Printed certificates template for graduation day

## 4. Before Day 1: facilitator prep

1. Test every session's live demo yourself, step by step, on the actual dataset — don't just read the script.
2. Print or share the [Assessment Rubrics](rubrics.md) so you know what "success" looks like before you teach.
3. Set up a WhatsApp or SMS group for the cohort for reminders and encouragement between sessions.
4. Decide your teaching pace: Level 1 assumes **zero prior Excel experience**. It is normal to spend extra time on Session 1.

## 5. Facilitator mindset

Many learners in this program have been told, directly or indirectly, that "data" and "computers" are not for them. Your job in the first hour is not to teach Excel — it's to prove that wrong. Be patient. Celebrate small wins loudly. No question is a stupid question.

## Level 1 Overview {#level-1-overview}


# Level 1 — Beginner Track Overview

## Who this is for
Women with **little or no prior Excel experience**. Some learners will be opening Excel for the first time. This level assumes nothing — not even what a "cell" is.

## Goal of Level 1
By the end of Level 1, a learner should be able to open a workbook, enter and format data correctly, sort and filter a list, clean up messy entries, build a proper Excel Table, write basic formulas, use simple IF logic, make a chart, apply conditional formatting, and produce a one-page report — using her **own real data** (savings group records, market sales, attendance registers, etc.)

2. [Session 2 — Data Entry & Formatting](session-02.md)

Each learner brings a small real dataset from her own life or work (market sales, savings group ledger, attendance list, household budget) and produces one clean, formatted sheet with at least one chart and one simple formula-based summary. See the [Level 1 Capstone Guide](../capstone/level1-capstone.md).

## Level 1 Sessions {#level-1-sessions}


# Session 1: Excel Interface & Workbook Navigation

**1. Session Title:** Excel Interface & Workbook Navigation

**2. Learning Goal:** By the end of this session, each learner can open Excel, identify the main parts of the screen, move around a worksheet, and save a workbook correctly.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation**
- Test that Excel opens on every laptop before learners arrive.
- Load `l1_attendance_register.csv` onto every laptop or a shared drive.
- Prepare a printed "map" of the Excel screen (Ribbon, Tabs, Cell, Row, Column, Name Box, Formula Bar) to hand out — many learners find labelled diagrams reassuring.

**5. Materials Needed**
- Laptop per learner (or pair)
- `l1_attendance_register.csv`
- Printed screen-map handout
- Projector for live demo

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_attendance_register.csv` — a simple workshop attendance register with learner names, states, and attendance marks across 4 sessions.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Open Excel and open a **new blank workbook**. Point out the Ribbon, Tabs (Home, Insert, etc.), the grid of rows and columns, the Name Box, and the Formula Bar.
2. Show how a cell is named by its column letter and row number (e.g. cell B3).
3. Click on different cells and show the Name Box changing.
4. Use arrow keys, Tab, and Enter to move between cells.
5. Open `l1_attendance_register.csv` and show how a workbook can have multiple **sheets** (tabs at the bottom).
6. Rename a sheet tab and change its colour.
7. Save the file using **Save As**, choosing a clear file name and location, in `.xlsx` format.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together, as a group: open `l1_attendance_register.csv`, click on the cell containing "Halima Yusuf," then use the Name Box to jump to cell D5. Everyone renames Sheet1 to "Attendance."

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Each learner opens a new blank workbook, renames Sheet1 to her own first name, types her name in cell A1, and saves the file as `[HerName]_Session1.xlsx` in a folder she can find again.

**10. Expected Output**
A saved `.xlsx` file with one renamed sheet containing the learner's name in cell A1.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Learner saves as `.csv` instead of `.xlsx` and loses formatting later. *Fix:* When using Save As, point out the "Save as type" dropdown and confirm it says Excel Workbook (*.xlsx).
- *Mistake:* Learner double-clicks and accidentally moves a cell's content. *Fix:* Show Ctrl+Z (Undo) immediately — tell learners Ctrl+Z is their best friend.
- *Mistake:* Learner cannot find where she saved the file. *Fix:* Always save to a labelled Desktop folder created at the start of the workshop, e.g. "Excel Workshop 2026."

**12. Reflection Questions**
- What part of the Excel screen was the most confusing at first?
- Can you now find a specific cell if I tell you its name (e.g. "go to C4")?

**13. Assignment**
Before next session: open Excel at home or on a friend's laptop and practice moving between at least 10 different cells using both the mouse and the arrow keys.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This session sets the emotional tone for the whole workshop. Do not rush. Some learners will need one-on-one help just to open Excel. Pair a faster learner with a slower one — this builds community and confidence on both sides.

# Session 2: Data Entry & Formatting

**1. Session Title:** Data Entry & Formatting

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can enter data accurately, format text and numbers, adjust column widths/row heights, and make a sheet easy to read.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation**
- Load `l1_market_sales.csv` onto every laptop.
- Prepare an example of a "messy" vs "well-formatted" sheet to show side by side.

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l1_market_sales.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_market_sales.csv` — daily market sales records from traders in Onitsha, Kano, and Kejetia markets.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Open `l1_market_sales.csv`. Show how numbers align right and text aligns left by default — explain why (Excel is telling you what it thinks each cell is).
2. Bold the header row and apply a background fill colour.
3. Widen columns using AutoFit (double-click the column border).
4. Format the "Total Sales (NGN)" column as Currency with no decimal places.
5. Format the "Date" column consistently using Format Cells.
6. Freeze the header row (View > Freeze Panes) so it stays visible while scrolling.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: bold and colour the header row, freeze it, and format the Unit Price column as currency.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners format the Total Sales column as currency, centre-align the Quantity Sold column, and apply a light fill colour to every other row for readability.

**10. Expected Output**
A neatly formatted market sales sheet: bold headers, frozen top row, currency formatting, and alternating row colours.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Typing "5,000" as text instead of a number. *Fix:* Show how text-numbers align left; retype using Format Cells > Number.
- *Mistake:* Formatting numbers as currency changes the value. *Fix:* Reassure learners formatting only changes appearance, not the underlying number.
- *Mistake:* Freeze Panes freezing the wrong row. *Fix:* Click the cell just below the row you want frozen before applying Freeze Panes.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- Why does Excel treat numbers and text differently?
- How does good formatting help someone reading your sheet for the first time?

**13. Assignment**
Format a small list of 10 items from home (e.g. household shopping list) with bold headers and currency formatting.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Emphasise that formatting is not decoration — it's communication. A well-formatted sheet is taken more seriously by supervisors and donors.

# Session 3: Sorting & Filtering

**1. Session Title:** Sorting & Filtering

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can sort data by one or more columns and use filters to view only the rows they need.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Load `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`. Prepare 3 sample questions learners must answer using sort/filter (e.g. "Who contributed the least in Week 2?").

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv` — weekly savings (ajo/esusu-style) contributions from a women's savings group across several towns.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Select the data range and apply Sort (Data tab > Sort) by "Contribution (NGN)" from smallest to largest.
2. Show Sort by multiple levels: Town (A–Z), then Contribution (largest to smallest).
3. Apply Filter (Data tab > Filter) and show the dropdown arrows appear on headers.
4. Filter to show only Week 2 rows.
5. Filter to show only "Cash" payment method.
6. Clear all filters.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: sort the sheet by Town then Week. Then filter to show only members from Lagos.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners must find and write down: (a) who paid the most in Week 3, (b) how many members used Mobile Money, (c) all rows where Notes is not blank.

**10. Expected Output**
Correct answers to the 3 questions, achieved using Sort and Filter (not by manually scanning).

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Selecting only one column before sorting, which scrambles the data. *Fix:* Always select the whole table, or use "Expand the selection" when prompted.
- *Mistake:* Forgetting a filter is still active, causing confusion later. *Fix:* Show the small funnel icon that appears on filtered columns as a visual reminder.
- *Mistake:* Sorting a Table with formulas incorrectly. *Fix:* Explain sort works safely within Excel Tables (covered in Session 5).

**12. Reflection Questions**
- How could sorting help you prepare a report for your savings group treasurer?
- What is the difference between sorting and filtering?

**13. Assignment**
Bring a small list of numbers/names from home or work and practice sorting it two different ways.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Use a real savings group scenario ("Who owes contribution this week?") to make the skill click emotionally, not just technically.

# Session 4: Basic Data Cleaning

**1. Session Title:** Basic Data Cleaning

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can spot and fix common data problems: inconsistent capitalization, extra spaces, blank cells, and simple typos.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv` (note "chidinma eze" lowercase vs "Chidinma Eze"). Highlight these inconsistencies beforehand so you can point them out live.

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv` — contains a genuine inconsistent name entry ("chidinma eze" vs "Chidinma Eze") and a blank cell (Fatima Bello, Week 2 absent).

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Point out "chidinma eze" typed in lowercase in Week 1 — explain Excel treats this as a different-looking entry than "Chidinma Eze," which can break sorting/lookups later.
2. Use the `PROPER()` function in a helper column to auto-capitalise names correctly.
3. Use `TRIM()` to remove extra spaces from a cell.
4. Use Find & Replace to standardise "Cash " (with trailing space) to "Cash."
5. Show how to identify blank cells using Ctrl+G > Special > Blanks.
6. Discuss: should a blank mean "zero" or "no data"? Decide together and fill consistently.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: create a helper column with `=PROPER(A2)` to fix all names, then copy-paste as values over the original column.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners find and fix all inconsistent entries in the Payment Method column, and decide how to handle the blank contribution for Fatima Bello in Week 2.

**10. Expected Output**
A cleaned dataset with consistent name capitalization, no stray spaces, and a clear decision recorded for blank cells.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Deleting the original data before checking the helper column is correct. *Fix:* Always keep a helper column temporarily; only delete the original after confirming with Paste Special > Values.
- *Mistake:* Confusing `TRIM()` with deleting the whole cell. *Fix:* Demonstrate that TRIM only removes extra spaces, not the text itself.
- *Mistake:* Leaving blanks without deciding what they mean. *Fix:* Always ask the group: does blank = 0, or blank = "not yet recorded"?

**12. Reflection Questions**
- Why can messy data cause wrong totals or lookup errors later?
- What's one habit you can build now to avoid messy data in your own records?

**13. Assignment**
Take any list you keep by hand (attendance, sales, contributions) and check it for capitalization and spacing inconsistencies.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This is the session where learners often say "ah, so THAT's why my totals were wrong before!" Let that realisation land — it builds strong motivation for the rest of the course.

# Session 5: Excel Tables

**1. Session Title:** Excel Tables

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can convert a plain data range into a proper Excel Table and understand why Tables are more reliable than plain ranges.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use the cleaned version of `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv` from Session 4.

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, cleaned dataset from Session 4, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** Cleaned `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Select the data range and press Ctrl+T (or Insert > Table).
2. Confirm "My table has headers."
3. Show the new banded rows, filter arrows, and Table Design tab.
4. Rename the Table (e.g. "SavingsData") in the Table Name box.
5. Add a new row at the bottom and show it auto-formats.
6. Add a Total Row and show it can Sum the Contribution column automatically.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: convert the dataset to a Table, rename it, and add a Total Row summing contributions.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners add 3 new rows of their own invented data to the bottom of the Table and confirm the Total Row updates automatically.

**10. Expected Output**
A named Excel Table with a working Total Row that updates when new data is added.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Typing new data just below the Table instead of inside it. *Fix:* Show the small blue corner handle that marks the Table's boundary — new rows must go inside it.
- *Mistake:* Believing Tables and Tables' formatting can't be changed. *Fix:* Show Table Design > Table Styles to pick a different look.
- *Mistake:* Confusing an Excel Table with a "table" in the everyday sense (any grid of data). *Fix:* Clarify: an Excel Table is a *named, structured* object with special powers (auto-expand, structured references, Total Row).

**12. Reflection Questions**
- How does a Table help when your savings group adds new members every month?
- What changed automatically when you added new rows?

**13. Assignment**
Convert one of your own real lists (attendance, contributions, sales) into a Table before the next session.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Tables are the foundation for Level 2 (PivotTables, lookups). Do not rush this session — a strong grasp here makes Level 2 much easier.

# Session 6: Basic Formulas & Functions

**1. Session Title:** Basic Formulas & Functions

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can write simple formulas (+, -, *, /) and use SUM, AVERAGE, MIN, MAX, and COUNT.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use `l1_market_sales.csv`. Prepare a simple explanation of "cell reference" vs "typing the number directly."

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l1_market_sales.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_market_sales.csv` — market sales by trader, item, quantity, and unit price.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Show that every formula starts with `=`.
2. Build `=Quantity*UnitPrice` manually in a new column to double check the Total Sales column.
3. Introduce `=SUM(range)` to total all sales.
4. Introduce `=AVERAGE(range)` to find average sale value.
5. Introduce `=MIN()` and `=MAX()` to find smallest and largest sales.
6. Introduce `=COUNT()` to count how many sales were recorded.
7. Show the difference between typing a number and clicking a cell reference — and why cell references are better (they update automatically).

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build a small summary box below the data showing Total Sales, Average Sale, Highest Sale, and Number of Transactions using the functions above.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners calculate: total quantity sold, average unit price, and the highest single sale — using formulas, not by reading and calculating manually.

**10. Expected Output**
A working summary box with 4 correct formula-based answers that update if the data changes.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Typing numbers directly into a formula instead of referencing cells. *Fix:* Show what happens when you change the original number — the direct-number formula doesn't update, but the cell-reference formula does.
- *Mistake:* Forgetting the `=` sign, so Excel just shows text. *Fix:* Remind learners every formula begins with `=`.
- *Mistake:* Selecting the wrong range in SUM/AVERAGE. *Fix:* Teach learners to click-and-drag to select the range instead of typing cell references from memory.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- Why is a formula better than a calculator for repeated work?
- Which of these 4 functions (SUM, AVERAGE, MIN, MAX) would be most useful in your own work?

**13. Assignment**
Using your own data (or the market sales file), calculate total, average, min, and max for any numeric column.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Some learners fear "formulas" as a scary word. Reframe it early: "A formula is just a question you're asking Excel to answer for you."

# Session 7: IF Statements & Simple Charts

**1. Session Title:** IF Statements & Simple Charts

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can write a basic IF formula to classify data, and build a simple column or pie chart.

**3. Duration:** 2.5 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`. Decide the IF logic in advance (e.g. "Full" if contribution = 2000, "Short" if less).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_savings_group_contributions.csv`.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Explain IF in plain language: "IF something is true, do this; otherwise, do that."
2. Build `=IF(D2>=2000,"Full Payment","Short Payment")` in a new column.
3. Copy the formula down the column and check results make sense.
4. Select the Town column and Contribution column (using Ctrl to select non-adjacent columns), then Insert > Column Chart.
5. Add a chart title and axis labels.
6. Build a simple Pie Chart showing total contribution by town.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: write the IF formula for Payment Status, then build one column chart showing contributions by member for Week 3.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners write their own IF formula (e.g. classify contributions as "Above Average" or "Below Average" using the AVERAGE from Session 6), then create a pie chart of contributions by town.

**10. Expected Output**
A working IF-based classification column and one properly labelled chart.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Missing quotation marks around text results in IF (e.g. `=IF(D2>=2000,Full,Short)`). *Fix:* Remind learners that any text inside a formula needs quotation marks.
- *Mistake:* Selecting the wrong data range for the chart, producing a confusing chart. *Fix:* Always select headers + data together, and check the chart preview before finalising.
- *Mistake:* Comparing text instead of numbers (e.g. contribution stored as text "2000" instead of number 2000). *Fix:* Confirm the column is truly numeric (right-aligned) before writing the IF formula.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- How could an IF formula help you flag which members are behind on contributions?
- Which chart type told the "story" of the data better — column or pie? Why?

**13. Assignment**
Build one IF formula and one chart using your own data before next session.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Charts often produce the biggest "wow" reaction of the whole workshop. Let learners enjoy this moment — it's a great confidence boost heading into the final session.

# Session 8: Conditional Formatting, Basic Reports & Capstone Launch

**1. Session Title:** Conditional Formatting, Basic Reports & Capstone: "Everyday Data Story"

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can apply conditional formatting to highlight important data, assemble a simple one-page report, and complete their Level 1 capstone project.

**3. Duration:** 3 hours (longer session — includes capstone work time and mini-presentations)

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Review the [Level 1 Capstone Guide](../capstone/level1-capstone.md) and [Assessment Rubric](../rubrics.md) before this session. Ask learners in Session 7 to bring a small real dataset from their own life or work.

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, learners' own small datasets (or `l1_women_enterprise_directory.csv` as backup), projector, printed rubric.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l1_women_enterprise_directory.csv` (backup/demo) or learner's own dataset.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Apply Conditional Formatting > Highlight Cell Rules > Greater Than, to highlight contributions above a chosen value in green.
2. Apply a Colour Scale to a numeric column to show a "heat map" style view.
3. Apply an Icon Set (e.g. arrows) to show up/down trends.
4. Assemble a simple one-page report: Table + one chart + one summary sentence typed in a cell, e.g. "Total sales this week: [formula result]."
5. Use Page Layout > Print Area and Page Setup to make the report print-ready on one page.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: apply a colour scale to the Years Active column in `l1_women_enterprise_directory.csv`, and build one combined report view (table + chart + one summary sentence).

**9. Independent Practice Task — Capstone Work Time**
Learners spend the rest of the session on their **"Everyday Data Story"** capstone: clean their own dataset (using Session 4 skills), format it (Session 2), sort/filter it (Session 3), build a Table (Session 5), add at least one formula (Session 6), one IF classification (Session 7), one chart (Session 7), and one conditional formatting rule (this session).

**10. Expected Output**
A completed one-page "Everyday Data Story": a clean, Table-based, formatted sheet with at least one chart, one IF formula, and one conditional formatting rule, plus a one-sentence written summary of what the data shows.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Applying too many conditional formatting rules, making the sheet confusing. *Fix:* Recommend no more than 1–2 rules per report for clarity.
- *Mistake:* Running out of time because cleaning wasn't done early. *Fix:* Remind learners to clean data first — everything else depends on it.
- *Mistake:* Capstone dataset is too large/complicated for the time available. *Fix:* Encourage learners to use a subset (e.g. one month, one location) if their real data is too big.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- What is the one story your data tells that you didn't notice before you cleaned and charted it?
- Which Level 1 skill do you think you'll use the most in real life?

**13. Assignment**
Finalise and save the capstone file as `[HerName]_EverydayDataStory.xlsx`, ready to present or submit. Bring it to the graduation/showcase session.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This is a celebration session, not a test. Let 2–3 volunteers briefly show their "Everyday Data Story" to the group. Use the [Assessment Rubric](../rubrics.md) to give warm, specific, constructive feedback — not just a score.

# Level 2 — Intermediate Track Overview

## Who this is for
Women who have completed Level 1 (or already know the basics: entering data, formatting, sorting/filtering, basic formulas). Level 2 moves into the tools that let a learner turn messy real-world data into decisions — the kind of Excel skill that gets you noticed at an NGO, cooperative, or small business.

## Goal of Level 2
By the end of Level 2, a learner should be able to clean a messy dataset (duplicates, split columns, Flash Fill), build dropdown-controlled data entry, look up information across sheets (VLOOKUP, XLOOKUP, INDEX-MATCH), summarise data with conditional functions (COUNTIF, SUMIF, AVERAGEIF, IFS, IFERROR), build a PivotTable and PivotChart, filter with Slicers, and assemble it all into a simple one-page Dashboard.

## Capstone: "Community Data in Action"
Each learner (or small team) takes a realistic, messier community dataset (NGO beneficiary list, cooperative sales records, microloan repayments) and delivers a cleaned dataset, at least two lookup/summary formulas, one PivotTable + PivotChart, and a one-page dashboard with a Slicer. See the [Level 2 Capstone Guide](../capstone/level2-capstone.md).

## Facilitator note
Level 2 learners can get discouraged when formulas don't work on the first try. Normalise the `#N/A` and `#VALUE!` errors early — tell them on Day 1 that **every Excel user sees these every day**, including you.

# Session 1: Remove Duplicates & Text-to-Columns

**1. Session Title:** Remove Duplicates & Text-to-Columns

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can identify and remove duplicate records safely, and split one column of combined text into separate columns.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Load `l2_ngo_beneficiaries_duplicates.csv` (contains 2 genuine duplicate rows) and `l2_full_names_to_split.csv` (surname-first names to split).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, both datasets above, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_ngo_beneficiaries_duplicates.csv` — an NGO beneficiary list with accidental duplicate entries (common when multiple field officers register the same person). `l2_full_names_to_split.csv` — names stored as "Surname Firstname" needing to be split.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Open `l2_ngo_beneficiaries_duplicates.csv` and manually point out the two duplicate rows (Beneficiary ID B001 and B002 appear twice).
2. Select the data, go to Data tab > Remove Duplicates, choose to check all columns, and run it. Show the confirmation message ("2 duplicate values found and removed").
3. Discuss: what if only SOME columns match, not all? Decide as a group what counts as a "true duplicate" (e.g. same Beneficiary ID + same Phone Number).
4. Open `l2_full_names_to_split.csv`. Select the Full Name column, go to Data tab > Text to Columns.
5. Choose "Delimited," select "Space" as the delimiter, and finish — showing the name split into two columns.
6. Rename the new columns "Surname" and "First Name."

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: run Remove Duplicates on the NGO beneficiary list and confirm the row count drops correctly. Then split the names file using Text to Columns.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners must decide, before running Remove Duplicates, which columns should be checked to define "duplicate," then apply it and report how many duplicates were found.

**10. Expected Output**
A cleaned beneficiary list with duplicates removed, and a names file with Surname and First Name in separate columns.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Running Remove Duplicates on the wrong selection (missing some columns), which removes rows that aren't really duplicates. *Fix:* Always select the FULL data range, headers included, before starting.
- *Mistake:* Not making a backup copy before removing duplicates — this is destructive and cannot always be undone after saving. *Fix:* Teach the habit: **Save a copy first**, always, before Remove Duplicates.
- *Mistake:* Choosing the wrong delimiter in Text to Columns (e.g. comma instead of space). *Fix:* Use the Preview window in the Text to Columns wizard before clicking Finish.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- Why might the same beneficiary get registered twice in real NGO fieldwork?
- What could go wrong if you remove duplicates without checking first?

**13. Assignment**
Find a real list (from a WhatsApp group, phone contacts, or club register) and check it for duplicate entries.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Stress the "backup before you clean" habit strongly here — this is the first genuinely destructive tool in the course. Make it a rule, not a suggestion.

# Session 2: Flash Fill & Data Validation / Dropdowns

**1. Session Title:** Flash Fill & Data Validation / Dropdowns

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can use Flash Fill to quickly reformat data based on a pattern, and build dropdown lists to control what can be entered in a cell.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use the split-name output from Session 1 (`l2_full_names_to_split.csv`) for Flash Fill practice, and `l2_microloan_repayments.csv` for dropdown practice (Status column).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, both datasets, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_full_names_to_split.csv` and `l2_microloan_repayments.csv` — a microloan repayment tracker for small women-owned businesses (tailoring, grocery, hairdressing, catering).

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. In a new column next to Surname/First Name, type the first correct example of "Firstname Surname" order by hand.
2. Press Enter, then start typing the next one — show Excel suggesting the rest (Flash Fill preview), and press Enter to accept, or Ctrl+E to trigger Flash Fill manually.
3. Open `l2_microloan_repayments.csv`. Select the Status column cells, go to Data tab > Data Validation.
4. Choose "List" and type the allowed values: Fully Paid, Partial, Not Paid.
5. Show how a dropdown arrow now appears, and typing anything else gives an error.
6. Show how to add an input message and error alert for friendliness.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: use Flash Fill to reformat all names to "Firstname Surname," then add a dropdown list to the Status column with the 3 allowed values.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners create a dropdown list for "Business Type" restricted to: Tailoring, Grocery Store, Hairdressing, Catering — and test that typing an invalid entry is blocked.

**10. Expected Output**
A reformatted names column (via Flash Fill) and a working dropdown-restricted Status/Business Type column.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Flash Fill doesn't trigger because the pattern wasn't consistent in the first 1-2 examples. *Fix:* Show a clean, consistent first example before letting Flash Fill guess.
- *Mistake:* Data Validation list typed with inconsistent commas or spaces. *Fix:* When typing the list source, ensure no extra spaces between comma-separated items.
- *Mistake:* Learner believes Data Validation fixes data that's already wrong. *Fix:* Clarify: Data Validation only controls **new** entries going forward — it doesn't clean what's already there.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- Where in your own work could a dropdown prevent typing mistakes (e.g. inconsistent business names)?
- How did Flash Fill save you time compared to retyping everything?

**13. Assignment**
Build one dropdown list for a real recurring category in your own work (e.g. payment method, attendance status).

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Dropdowns are a favourite "aha" moment for learners who manage group records — connect it directly to reducing data entry errors in their savings groups or businesses.

# Session 3: VLOOKUP & XLOOKUP

**1. Session Title:** VLOOKUP & XLOOKUP

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can look up information from one table into another using both VLOOKUP and the newer XLOOKUP function.

**3. Duration:** 2.5 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` alongside `l2_lookup_reference_table.csv` (Product Code/Category/Standard Price reference).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, both datasets, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` — sales orders from women's cooperatives in Nairobi and Mombasa, and `l2_lookup_reference_table.csv` — a clean reference table of product codes and standard prices.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Explain the goal: "I want Excel to find the Standard Price automatically, instead of me typing it in by hand."
2. Build `=VLOOKUP(lookup_value, table_array, col_index_num, FALSE)` step by step: pick the Product name as the lookup value, select the reference table, count columns to find col_index_num, and always use FALSE for exact match.
3. Show a common error: forgetting FALSE, which causes wrong/approximate matches.
4. Introduce XLOOKUP: `=XLOOKUP(lookup_value, lookup_array, return_array)` — simpler because there's no column counting.
5. Compare both side by side on the same task, discussing when XLOOKUP may not be available (older Excel versions).

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build one VLOOKUP formula to pull Standard Price into the cooperative sales sheet, then rebuild the same result using XLOOKUP.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners add a "Category" column to the sales data and use either VLOOKUP or XLOOKUP to pull it in automatically from the reference table.

**10. Expected Output**
A sales sheet with an automatically-filled Category (or Price) column, matched correctly for every row.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Forgetting to lock the reference table range with `$` (absolute reference) when copying the formula down, causing it to shift and break. *Fix:* Teach F4 to quickly add `$` signs, and show what happens without it.
- *Mistake:* Using TRUE (approximate match) instead of FALSE (exact match), returning wrong results. *Fix:* Repeat the rule: "For name/code lookups, always use FALSE."
- *Mistake:* Lookup value has extra spaces or different capitalization than the reference table. *Fix:* Revisit TRIM/PROPER from Level 1 Session 4 — clean data enables reliable lookups.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- Why do lookups fail so often when data isn't clean — what does this tell you about the order of Excel skills?
- When would you prefer XLOOKUP over VLOOKUP, if you have the choice?

**13. Assignment**
Build one lookup formula connecting two of your own real lists (e.g. member names to phone numbers, or product to price).

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This session is often where learners feel like "real" data analysts for the first time. Expect `#N/A` errors — treat them as a normal debugging step, not a failure.

# Session 4: INDEX-MATCH

**1. Session Title:** INDEX-MATCH

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners understand why INDEX-MATCH is more flexible than VLOOKUP and can build a basic INDEX-MATCH formula.

**3. Duration:** 2 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Reuse `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` and `l2_lookup_reference_table.csv`. Prepare one example where VLOOKUP would fail (looking up a column to the LEFT of the lookup column) to show INDEX-MATCH's advantage.

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, both datasets, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` and `l2_lookup_reference_table.csv`.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Show the limitation: VLOOKUP can only look to the right of the lookup column. Set up a scenario where you need to look up Product Code from Product Name (Code is to the left in the reference table) — VLOOKUP struggles here.
2. Introduce `MATCH(lookup_value, lookup_array, 0)` alone first — show it returns a *position number*, not the value itself.
3. Introduce `INDEX(return_array, row_number)` alone — show it returns a value from a given position.
4. Combine them: `=INDEX(return_array, MATCH(lookup_value, lookup_array, 0))`.
5. Use this combined formula to solve the "look to the left" problem that VLOOKUP couldn't.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build one INDEX-MATCH formula that looks up Product Code using Product Name, where Code sits to the left of Name in the reference table.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners build their own INDEX-MATCH formula to pull Standard Price using Product Name, and compare the result to the VLOOKUP version from Session 3.

**10. Expected Output**
A correct INDEX-MATCH formula producing the same result as VLOOKUP, but demonstrated on a "look left" scenario VLOOKUP can't handle.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Mixing up the order of arguments inside MATCH. *Fix:* Read the formula aloud as a sentence: "Find [this value] inside [this list], give me an exact match (0)."
- *Mistake:* Forgetting the `0` (exact match) inside MATCH. *Fix:* Repeat the same discipline as VLOOKUP's FALSE — always specify exact match for name/code lookups.
- *Mistake:* Believing INDEX-MATCH is "harder" and avoiding it. *Fix:* Reassure learners it's normal to find this the hardest formula in the course — mastery comes with repetition, not immediate ease.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- What is one situation in your own work where VLOOKUP would fail but INDEX-MATCH would work?
- Do you feel more confident with VLOOKUP or INDEX-MATCH right now — and why?

**13. Assignment**
Try rebuilding your Session 3 lookup formula using INDEX-MATCH instead, and note any differences you notice.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This is the most conceptually demanding session in Level 2. It's fine to spend extra time here and revisit briefly in Session 5. Do not skip the "why" — understanding beats memorising.

# Session 5: COUNTIF, SUMIF, AVERAGEIF, IFS, IFERROR

**1. Session Title:** COUNTIF, SUMIF, AVERAGEIF, IFS, IFERROR

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can summarise data based on conditions, handle multiple conditions with IFS, and catch formula errors gracefully with IFERROR.

**3. Duration:** 2.5 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use `l2_microloan_repayments.csv` — a good dataset for counting/summing by Status and Business Type.

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l2_microloan_repayments.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_microloan_repayments.csv` — microloan tracker for women-owned small businesses.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Build `=COUNTIF(range,"Fully Paid")` to count how many loans are fully repaid.
2. Build `=SUMIF(range,"Fully Paid",amount_range)` to total the value of fully repaid loans.
3. Build `=AVERAGEIF(range,"Tailoring",loan_range)` to find average loan size for tailoring businesses.
4. Introduce `IFS` for more than 2 outcomes: `=IFS(D2=0,"Not Started",D2<E2,"Partial",D2>=E2,"Complete")` to classify repayment progress.
5. Introduce `IFERROR` by intentionally creating a lookup error from Session 3, then wrapping it: `=IFERROR(VLOOKUP(...),"Not Found")`.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build COUNTIF and SUMIF formulas to summarise loans by Status, then wrap one lookup formula in IFERROR to replace `#N/A` with a friendly message.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners calculate: (a) total amount repaid by business type using SUMIF, (b) average loan amount for "Grocery Store" businesses using AVERAGEIF, (c) an IFS formula classifying each loan as "Fully Paid," "Partial," or "Not Paid" based on amounts (cross-checking against the existing Status column).

**10. Expected Output**
Working COUNTIF, SUMIF, AVERAGEIF, IFS, and at least one IFERROR-wrapped formula, all producing sensible results.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Typing the condition without quotation marks (e.g. `=COUNTIF(range,Fully Paid)`). *Fix:* Remind learners text conditions always need quotation marks.
- *Mistake:* Mismatched range sizes between the condition range and the sum/average range in SUMIF/AVERAGEIF. *Fix:* Always check both ranges start and end on the same rows.
- *Mistake:* Using IFERROR to "hide" a real problem instead of fixing it. *Fix:* Teach IFERROR as a *last step* for genuinely expected issues (e.g. "not found" cases) — not a way to avoid understanding why a formula fails.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- How could COUNTIF/SUMIF help your NGO or cooperative report to donors monthly, without manual counting?
- When is it appropriate to use IFERROR versus fixing the root cause of an error?

**13. Assignment**
Build one COUNTIF or SUMIF formula summarising a real category in your own work.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
These "conditional" functions are some of the most immediately useful skills in the whole course for report-writing. Connect explicitly to real reporting tasks learners already do by hand.

# Session 6: PivotTables

**1. Session Title:** PivotTables

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can build a basic PivotTable to summarise large data by category without writing formulas.

**3. Duration:** 2.5 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` — good size and structure for a first PivotTable (Cooperative, Region, Product, Month, Units Sold).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` — sales orders across 3 cooperatives, multiple products, and 3 months.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Convert the data range to a Table first (Ctrl+T) — reinforce the Level 1 Session 5 habit.
2. Insert > PivotTable, placing it on a new worksheet.
3. Drag "Cooperative" to Rows, "Units Sold" to Values (confirm it shows Sum, not Count).
4. Drag "Month" to Columns to see units sold by cooperative, broken down by month.
5. Add "Product" to Filters, and show how filtering to one product changes the whole table.
6. Rename Value field labels to something clear, e.g. "Sum of Units Sold" → "Total Units Sold."

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build a PivotTable showing Total Units Sold by Cooperative and Month, then add Product as a filter.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners build their own PivotTable showing Total Sales Value (Units Sold × Unit Price — may need a helper column first) by Region and Category.

**10. Expected Output**
A working PivotTable correctly summarising sales data by at least two dimensions (e.g. Cooperative and Month).

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Source data isn't a proper Table, so the PivotTable doesn't auto-update with new rows. *Fix:* Always convert to a Table before building a PivotTable.
- *Mistake:* Values area shows "Count" instead of "Sum" because of one blank or text cell in the column. *Fix:* Check the column for stray blanks/text, clean it, then click Value Field Settings to change Count to Sum.
- *Mistake:* Learner feels overwhelmed by the Field List. *Fix:* Teach the 4 boxes one at a time (Filters, Rows, Columns, Values) using the plain-language framing: "Rows = what to group by, Values = what to measure."

**12. Reflection Questions**
- What would take you the longest to calculate by hand that a PivotTable just did in seconds?
- Which two categories in your own data would you most want to compare with a PivotTable?

**13. Assignment**
Build a PivotTable using two of your own real categories (e.g. contributions by member and by month).

**14. Facilitator Notes**
Expect a mix of excitement and intimidation. Slow down when introducing the Field List — this is often the single biggest confidence-building moment of Level 2 once it clicks.

# Session 7: PivotCharts & Slicers

**1. Session Title:** PivotCharts & Slicers

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can turn a PivotTable into a visual PivotChart and add Slicers for easy, button-based filtering.

**3. Duration:** 2.5 hours

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Use the PivotTable built in Session 6 from `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` (or rebuild fresh at the start of class).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv`, projector.

**6. Sample Dataset:** `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv`.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. Click inside the existing PivotTable, go to PivotTable Analyze > PivotChart, choose a Column Chart.
2. Show how the chart updates instantly if the PivotTable's fields change.
3. Go to PivotTable Analyze > Insert Slicer, select "Region" and "Product."
4. Click different Slicer buttons and show both the PivotTable and PivotChart updating together.
5. Format the Slicer (resize, choose a colour) so it's easy to use for a non-technical audience.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build a PivotChart showing Units Sold by Month, add a Region Slicer, and test filtering to "Mombasa" only.

**9. Independent Practice Task**
Learners add a Product Slicer to their own Session 6 PivotTable/Chart and test filtering to a single product.

**10. Expected Output**
A PivotChart connected to at least one working Slicer that filters both the chart and the table together.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Slicer appears not to filter the chart. *Fix:* Confirm the Slicer is connected to the same PivotTable the chart is based on (Slicer > Report Connections).
- *Mistake:* Chart type chosen doesn't fit the data (e.g. Pie chart with too many categories, becoming unreadable). *Fix:* Recommend Column charts for comparisons across more than 4–5 categories.
- *Mistake:* Learner resizes the Slicer awkwardly, covering the chart. *Fix:* Show drag-handles to resize and reposition calmly.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- How would a Slicer help a cooperative leader explore sales data herself, without needing you to rebuild the report each time?
- What's the difference between a normal chart (Session 7, Level 1) and a PivotChart?

**13. Assignment**
Practice building one PivotChart + Slicer combination using your own PivotTable data.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This session often produces genuine delight — learners see how a "self-service" report feels. Emphasise this is exactly what Session 8's Dashboard will bring together.

# Session 8: Dashboards & Capstone: "Community Data in Action"

**1. Session Title:** Dashboards & Capstone: "Community Data in Action"

**2. Learning Goal:** Learners can assemble a simple one-page dashboard combining a PivotTable, PivotChart, and Slicer, and complete their Level 2 capstone project.

**3. Duration:** 3 hours (longer session — includes capstone work time and presentations)

**4. Facilitator Preparation:** Review the [Level 2 Capstone Guide](../capstone/level2-capstone.md) and [Assessment Rubric](../rubrics.md). Ask learners in Session 7 to bring or choose a real community dataset (NGO, cooperative, or microloan-style data).

**5. Materials Needed:** Laptop per learner, `l2_ngo_beneficiaries_duplicates.csv` / `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv` / `l2_microloan_repayments.csv` (learner's choice or own data), projector, printed rubric.

**6. Sample Dataset:** Learner's chosen dataset, or any of the Level 2 practice datasets as backup.

**7. Live Demonstration Steps**
1. On a new sheet, place a PivotTable, its PivotChart, and a Slicer together in one clean layout.
2. Remove gridlines (View > Gridlines, unchecked) for a cleaner dashboard look.
3. Add a title text box, e.g. "Umoja Women Coop — Monthly Sales Dashboard."
4. Add one or two KPI-style cells using SUMIF/COUNTIF from Session 5 (e.g. "Total Fully Paid Loans: [formula]") next to the PivotChart.
5. Test the Slicer live to confirm everything updates together.

**8. Guided Hands-On Activity**
Together: build one shared example dashboard combining all Level 2 skills, using `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv`.

**9. Independent Practice Task — Capstone Work Time**
Learners spend the rest of the session on their **"Community Data in Action"** capstone: clean their dataset (Session 1), validate entry with dropdowns where relevant (Session 2), build at least one lookup formula (Session 3 or 4), one conditional summary formula (Session 5), one PivotTable (Session 6), one PivotChart with Slicer (Session 7), and assemble it into a one-page Dashboard (this session).

**10. Expected Output**
A completed one-page Dashboard: PivotTable + PivotChart + Slicer + at least one KPI formula + a short title, built from a cleaned, validated dataset.

**11. Common Mistakes and Fixes**
- *Mistake:* Dashboard becomes cluttered with too many charts/elements. *Fix:* Recommend a maximum of 1 PivotChart + 1–2 KPI cells + 1 Slicer for a first dashboard.
- *Mistake:* Underlying data isn't fully cleaned, so the PivotTable groups the same category twice (e.g. "cash" and "Cash"). *Fix:* Revisit Level 1 Session 4 cleaning steps before finalising.
- *Mistake:* Running out of time. *Fix:* Encourage learners to prioritise: cleaned data + one PivotTable + one chart is a complete, presentable capstone even without every optional element.

**12. Reflection Questions**
- What decision could someone in your community or organisation make faster because of this dashboard?
- Looking back at both Level 1 and Level 2, which single skill changed how you think about your own data the most?

**13. Assignment**
Finalise and save the capstone file as `[HerName]_CommunityDataInAction.xlsx`, ready to present at the graduation/showcase session.

**14. Facilitator Notes**
This is the final session — make it a genuine celebration. Invite 2–3 volunteers to present their dashboards to the group. Award certificates. Use the [Assessment Rubric](../rubrics.md) for structured, encouraging feedback, and remind learners that Excel skill grows with continued real-world use, not just workshop attendance.

## Level 1 Capstone Guide {#level-1-capstone-guide}


# Level 1 Capstone: "Everyday Data Story"

## Purpose
Every learner turns a small piece of her own real life or work into a clean, well-presented Excel sheet — proving she can use every Level 1 skill together, not just in isolation.

## What learners need to bring
A small dataset (10–30 rows is plenty) from their own life or work, for example:
- Market or shop sales records
- Savings group (ajo/esusu/chama) contribution log
- Household budget or shopping list
- Attendance register from church, mosque, or community group
- A small business's customer or order list

If a learner has no data of her own, she may use `l1_women_enterprise_directory.csv` as a backup.

## Requirements checklist
The final workbook must include:
- [ ] Data entered cleanly with consistent formatting (Session 2)
- [ ] Data sorted and/or filtered at least once, with results noted (Session 3)
- [ ] Common cleaning issues fixed — capitalization, spacing, blanks (Session 4)
- [ ] Data converted into a proper Excel Table with a Total Row (Session 5)
- [ ] At least one basic formula: SUM, AVERAGE, MIN, MAX, or COUNT (Session 6)
- [ ] One IF formula classifying rows into two categories (Session 7)
- [ ] One chart (column or pie) with a title and labels (Session 7)
- [ ] One conditional formatting rule (Session 8)
- [ ] One written sentence summarising what the data shows

## Presentation format
2–3 minutes per learner (or a written summary if time is short): show the sheet, explain one insight the data revealed, and name one Excel skill she's proudest of using.

## Facilitator scoring
Use the [Assessment Rubric](../rubrics.md) — Level 1 Capstone section.

## Level 2 Capstone Guide {#level-2-capstone-guide}


# Level 2 Capstone: "Community Data in Action"

## Purpose
Learners (individually or in small teams of 2–3) take a realistic, messier community dataset and deliver a cleaned, analysed, and visualised one-page dashboard — the kind of deliverable that would genuinely help an NGO, cooperative, or small business make a decision.

## What learners need to bring or choose
A realistic dataset with at least 20–50 rows, for example:
- NGO beneficiary or distribution records
- Cooperative or small business sales data
- Microloan or savings repayment tracker
- Community health, education, or attendance data

If a learner has no suitable data, she may use `l2_cooperative_sales_data.csv`, `l2_ngo_beneficiaries_duplicates.csv`, or `l2_microloan_repayments.csv` as a backup.

## Requirements checklist
The final workbook must include:
- [ ] Duplicates identified and removed, with a note on what counted as "duplicate" (Session 1)
- [ ] At least one Data Validation dropdown used correctly (Session 2)
- [ ] At least one lookup formula: VLOOKUP, XLOOKUP, or INDEX-MATCH (Session 3–4)
- [ ] At least one conditional summary formula: COUNTIF, SUMIF, AVERAGEIF, or IFS (Session 5)
- [ ] At least one IFERROR-wrapped formula (Session 5)
- [ ] One PivotTable summarising the data by at least two categories (Session 6)
- [ ] One PivotChart connected to at least one Slicer (Session 7)
- [ ] A one-page Dashboard combining the above with a clear title and at least one KPI cell (Session 8)
