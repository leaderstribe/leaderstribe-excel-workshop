# Facilitator Tips

Practical, classroom-tested advice for running this workshop well.

## Before the workshop
- Test every demo yourself on the actual dataset, on the actual version of Excel your learners will use — screenshots and memory are not enough.
- Assume variable internet and power. Everything in this course works fully offline once the CSV files are loaded.
- Print a simple one-page "cheat sheet" of keyboard shortcuts (Ctrl+Z, Ctrl+C, Ctrl+V, Ctrl+T, Ctrl+E) for Session 1.

## Pacing
- Level 1, Session 1 always takes longer than planned. Budget extra time and don't feel behind.
- If more than a third of the room is stuck, stop and re-teach live rather than circulating one-on-one — it's more respectful of everyone's time.
- Build in a 10-minute break every 60–75 minutes; screen fatigue is real, especially for first-time computer users.

## Classroom culture
- Use first names and celebrate small wins loudly — "Yes! That's exactly right!" matters more here than in a technical bootcamp.
- Normalise mistakes explicitly: tell learners on Day 1 that you (the facilitator) still make typing and formula errors every week.
- Pair confident learners with nervous ones for hands-on activities — this builds peer teaching and community, which often outlasts the workshop itself.
- Watch for learners who go quiet rather than ask for help. Check in directly rather than waiting for a raised hand.

## Managing mixed skill levels in one room
- Give faster finishers an "extra challenge" line from the session page (most sessions can be stretched with a harder version of the independent task).
- Never let a faster learner's pace set the tone for the room — teach to the median, support the edges individually.

## Technology troubleshooting
- Keep a small "toolkit": 2 spare mice, one extension board, one set of HDMI/VGA adapters, and a printed copy of every dataset (in case a laptop fails on the day).
- If Excel versions differ across laptops (some older, no XLOOKUP), always demo VLOOKUP as the primary method and mention XLOOKUP as "if your Excel has it."

## Emotional tone matters as much as content
Many learners arrive believing "I'm not a computer person" or "I'm not good with numbers." Your real curriculum in the first two sessions is proving that belief wrong — the Excel skills follow naturally once that belief shifts.
